The Layers Panel

Now that your document is created and your workspace is set up correctly, we can explore one of the most important organizing tools in Adobe Illustrator: layers. Using the Layers panel not only helps you to organize your work, but allows others to easily navigate through your project when sharing files.

A single layer is conceptually a transparent sheet, allowing you to add content to as many layers or “transparency sheets” as you would like, while being able to see the content on other layers as well. You can then reorder and rename layers, hide them, or lock them to aid work flow on your project.

To change the title of the layer, double-click on the title and rename the layer.

The two boxes to the left of each layer name indicate visibility and locking of the layers. Clicking in the first of the two boxes toggles the visibility of a layer. This is useful when you want to focus on specific layers without being distracted by others.

Clicking in the second of the two boxes locks or unlocks a layer. When you lock a layer, you are unable to make any changes to that layer. It is useful to lock layers that you want to leave unadjusted, such as the background, while you work on the rest of your document.

Understanding Graphic Formats

There are many computer-generated graphic formats. Choosing the wrong type of file format when creating a graphic can affect the size and quality of graphics. A few formats are popular and widely accepted by many programs. For printing, the two most widely used graphic formats are TIFF and EPS. JPEG and GIF are not advised for printed publications. The following is an explanation of the four types:

TIFF stands for Tagged Image File Format. Like most graphic formats, these files are bitmap graphics (based on pixels). TIFF files can be in black and white, grayscale, or full color. If a TIFF is enlarged, the graphic quality may be reduced. TIFF files are best suited for graphics that are scanned into the computer or created with Adobe Photoshop.

EPS stands for Encapsulated PostScript. Graphics in the EPS file format may contain vector graphics (based on objects) as well as bitmap graphics. They can also be in black and white, grayscale, or full color. These graphics can be imported into almost all page layout and word processing programs, and they can be enlarged without sacrificing graphic quality. Graphics created with a vector-based drawing system, such as Adobe Illustrator or Freehand, should be saved in this format.

GIF and JPEG stand for Graphic Interchange Format and Joint Photographic Experts Group, respectively. These formats were designed with the World Wide Web in mind, so they are much smaller in file size than TIFF and EPS files. However, GIF and JPEG files do not print out as well as TIFF and EPS files. Therefore, avoid using GIF and JPEG files in print publications.

Understanding Image Resolution

Both printed and digital graphics are actually made up of tiny dots that hold color information. When the dots are blended together, they form a picture. Image resolution refers to the number of dots per square inch, or dpi, of the image file. High resolution images are sharper or clearer than low resolution images. High resolution digital images are also larger in file size.

Controlling image resolution is especially important for TIFF files. If an image is scanned in at a low resolution, enlarging the image will result in poor image quality. However, scanning images in at a very high resolution makes file sizes very large. A good rule of thumb is to use a resolution of 150 dpi for line art and 200 dpi for photographic images. This will give a good balance between acceptable print quality and manageable file size. However, for printing large photos on glossy paper, an image resolution between 300 and 600 dpi is recommended.

Although images may be resized when they reach a publication, excessive shrinking or enlarging of graphics produces undesirable results. If a small picture is over-stretched, the pixels themselves become overblown and the picture looks jagged. If a large picture is reduced after it is imported into a publication, the result may look fine but the file size of the graphic remains unnecessarily large.

Each line of text is independent, with the line expanding or shrinking as you type, but does not return to the next line. Tracking and Leading

Tracking controls the white space between characters in an entire block of text. Tracking and kerning are typographic terms that have to do with the space between characters.The default tracking settings for body text are usually adequate, but larger display text can often be adjusted for presentation purposes. Patterns

Patterns are a group of vector objects with rectangular borders, called a tile. This source tile is saved as a pattern. The source tile can be edited in the Patterns panel. To form the seamless pattern texture, the saved source is automatically tiled horizontally and vertically.

The tile direction is guided by rulers in the Illustrator document. Patterns usually start from the lower-left corner of the document. When the pattern is applied to a vector object as a fill or stroke, the result is a pixel-based image. However, it is still scalable because the source title is vector based, so the quality remains intact.

Best Practices

Congratulations on creating two projects in Adobe Illustrator! It is now up to you to use the skills you have learned in this and other Illustrator classes to keep making awesome designs. Here are some things to consider:

Be consistent with your project's color and style palettes.

Adding drop shadows, glow effects, or strokes to elements of your design makes them more visible and attention-grabbing. This is a good technique to create a focal point in your poster’s composition.

Gradients can add a lot of interest to a design, especially when applied to backgrounds. Be careful, though, that they do not decrease the readability of your poster.

Speaking of readability, keep in mind the size of your project. A postcard will be viewed close up, while a flyer or poster will most often be viewed from 10 to 20 feet away. Make sure your text is a reasonable size and remember to use a readable typeface.

Appendix: Advanced Image Trace

The Image Trace panel has many more options for you to use, and this section will go over the advanced image trace options in more detail.

To access the Advanced Options, Click the arrow next to “Advanced” to extend the panel window.

The first category is Paths. This determines the detail of the artwork. If you drag the slider to the left, the image loosley adheres to the image. If you drag the slider to the right, the paths of the artwork tighten and follow the contours of the original artwork more closely.

The next category is Corners. This determines the number of corners in the paths of the artwork. If you drag the slider to the right, the number of corners in the artwork increases. This can sometimes leave jagged lines and extra white space. If you drag the slider to the left, the paths smooth out, especially in detailed areas like around a face structure or hairlines.

The next category is Noise. This determines the amount of detail, or noise, the image contains. The higher the number of pixels, the less noise in the photo and the smaller details are ignored. The lower the number of pixels, the more noise in the photo and smaller details in the image are picked up.

The next category is Method. This determines how the paths interact with each other in the image. The first square represents Abutting paths, which are paths that are cut out and fit right along each others’ edges like a puzzle. The second square represents Overlapping paths, which are solid shapes stacked on each other in layers. The most useful time to use the overlapping paths option is when you will be moving around parts of the image and do not want any white space between your pieces.

The next two categories, Create and Stroke, are used to edit the stroke and fills of the image. You can select either fills or stroke and then select the maximum width in pixels.

The last category is the Options. You have the option of snapping the curves to lines, which would make all curved lines in the image straight. This is not particularly useful for all images because it can create gaps in the artwork. The other option is to ignore white, which ignores white parts of the picture and turns them transparent. This can be useful for single color logos scanned in on a piece of paper or other images where you do not want a white background.
